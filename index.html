Introduction

When we hear concerns about TikTok keeping people doom scrolling for hours right before bed or as a form of procrastination we often list these worries as insignificant events, but algorithmic addiction isn't something new. TikTok perfected techniques that have been developed over the past two decades by social media companies that have prioritized user attention over user wellbeing.

This exhibit traces the evolution of social media platform's algorithmic recommendation systems from 2009 to 2016 to reveal how Facebook, Instagram, and YouTube gradually refined features that now TikTok uses. Furthermore, research shows that concerns about TikTok on young adults include negative mental health impacts, sleep disruption, and reduced focus issues lacking of both impulse control and recall of specific information (Haque, 2026). These algorithms didn't appear overnight each platform experimented and eventually, despite user resistance, user control was replaced with platform control.

Additionally, in 2009 Facebook introduced EdgeRank which was a major shift from chronological to algorithmic feeds. This shift was framed as a solution to the "information overload" Facebook faced with its growing user base, but they never mentioned the gain they would achieve with the new adoption in relation to advertising revenue. Three years after YouTube replaced their view based algorithm to watch time based recommendation algorithm as a solution to prevent the high amounts of clickbait caused by their previous algorithm. Subsequently, Instagram also followed this shift in 2016 by abandoning their chronological feeds despite a petition with over 340,000 signatures. The pattern became clear that social media platforms chose to impose the engagement maximizing algorithms regardless of user opposition.

Moving on, researchers have debated whether such algorithmic systems produce "filter bubbles" and "polarization" (Berman and Katona, 2020) what's often is missing from these discussions is user perspective. A study documented that Facebook's algorithm created gaps between what users want to see and what the platform showed them, participants were prompted to notice posts from Facebook friends they had missed (Rader, 2017), which lead to frustration as users had no power to change such system.

In conclusion, this exhibit shows that due to the user resistance movements, platform announcements, and media coverage the outcome of this business model has prioritized engagement and ad revenue over user well being. Understanding the historical origin is essential to address the harms that are caused due to the algorithmic recommendation systems, because if the blame continues to be thrown at individual platforms and ignores the "attention economy" we are in, users will repeat the same mistakes with whatever app comes next.
